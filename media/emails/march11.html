LU Among 50 New Organizations Chosen for Google's Summer of Code

This summer, Google will pay a small team of college students from around the world to work with Learning Unlimited on pressing software needs to make our programs run more smoothly. Our Web-based platform provides the automation needed for small teams of college students to run programs that scale to hundreds of classes and thousands of participants. LU volunteers suggested nine substantial improvements frequently requested by our chapters, and invited applicants to propose their own ideas as well.

Other Summer of Code beneficiaries include the Wikimedia Foundation and research institutes at the University of Michigan and the University of Oregon. Among these, LU stands out as one of just three education-focused nonprofit organizations to receive such support.

"There's a lot of life in our software development community now, and that's great news for our chapters," said Dan Zaharopol, CEO of LU. "With this support we're going to be able to provide a better website package so that everyone's experience can be much smoother."

LU's software is completely open source, and contributions from the Summer of Code will be publicly accessible to other groups and individuals who want to use the software or improve upon it.

A Splash-filled Season

Students in an MIT Splash class.

Granite Hills High School in California was the first high school to run an LU-affiliated program. STING, on Saturday, March 5, attracted over 190 middle school students. STING received much local attention and you can find links on our blog.

There were few open slots at MIT Spark on Saturday, March 12, possibly the only place on earth that day (or in the Boston area, at least) where suggested class prerequisites ranged from "linear algebra and multivariable calculus" to deep familiarity with all six Star Wars movies and at least 5 Star Wars novels. LU ran a Parents' Program, and you can read our schedule and see our slides here.

NYU's cSplash, a math-focused program, featured classes on everything from "The Mathematics of Voting Systems" to "Time Travel and the Universe." Plus, there were a bunch of people in the 13th-floor lounge constructing giant polyhedra with spaghetti and marshmallows.

Boston College Splash ran its second successful program, attracting over 300 students from as far as Vermont and Maine. The Boston College Heights, a student-run newspaper, ran an article about the program.

Upcoming programs are scheduled at Northwestern University; the University of Maryland, Baltimore County; Stanford University; and Southwestern University (a pilot program recruiting from just one high school).
