Three Ways to Deepen Play

Tips inspired by Free To Learn by Peter Gray

1. Minimize adult supervision. In his book, Gray argues that play is vital to a child’s social and intellectual development as well as a sense of independence and enjoyment. Kids have most fun problem-solving, socializing, and negotiating with other kids; adult intervention makes the activity less meaningful.

2. “Fooling around” is important. If a child likes to play piano or solve puzzles in an unstructured way, this isn’t necessarily a cue that a child wants to take lessons in math or music. “The child feels that this is my space, not my parents’ space, and parents can nurture an interest to death,” Gray said in an interview. “If it’s really a passion for the child, the child will initiate developing it further.”

3. Make class playful by asking questions. Students can come up with questions about the material they are learning, even if those questions aren’t related to the topic. And for students who don’t like the topic? “I’ve seen students who ask themselves, “What about this topic makes my professor so passionate to teach it?’ If students can understand why somebody else likes a topic, it can help them understand why they could like it too,” Gray said.
