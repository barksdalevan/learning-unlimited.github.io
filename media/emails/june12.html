MIT Recognizes LU Volunteer for Leadership

MIT president Susan Hockfield congratulates MIT senior and LU volunteer Paul Kominers.
Photo credit: Justin Knight

MIT senior Paul Kominers was one of three students to win MIT’s Karl Taylor Compton Prize. Students are selected for the award based on leadership and contributions to the MIT community.

Paul co-directed MIT Spark as a freshman and co-directed MIT Splash as a sophomore, and continued his involvement as co-chair of the MIT Educational Studies Program, the student organization that manages MIT Spark and MIT Splash, and other programs.

As a leader, Paul managed college student volunteers who were serving several thousand teenagers throughout the school year. “This becomes like a part-time job for some of us.”

Paul is currently the Director of New Chapter Mentoring for Learning Unlimited, where he helps identify and develop future college student leaders. He has also mentored the Yale Splash and Babson Splash leaders who ran their first programs this year with support from LU.

“Since LU, it’s been a livelier world for college student volunteers running a Splash program.”

MIT Splash teacher to speak alongside Farmville and PayPal founders at “Hackademic Camp”

David Dalrymple

By the time most teens have a drivers license, MIT Splash teacher David Dalrymple already earned a masters degree from MIT.

Then, David did the truly unthinkable: he dropped out of his graduate program at MIT, risking his connections to scholars and support for his research projects, to join Professor Sharad Ramanathan at Harvard, where he is building mathematical models of nervous systems, among other projects.

“I want to understand everything as some kind of mathematical concept,” David said.

The Hackademic Camp, which describes itself as “an intense one-week human accelerator,” intends to bring together teenagers to entrepreneurs, filmmakers, software developers, and academics with non traditional backgrounds.

David reflected on the advice he intends to give campers: “Don’t be afraid to challenge the system from within. If you have an idea for a project that isn’t related to your school work, let the leaders in on the idea and get them interested in what you’re interested in.”
